Step into it's history

The history of web servers goes all the way back to 1989, when Tim Berners-Lee submitted his initial ideas for what the World Wide Web would become, and in 1990, a team came together with him to build the world's first browser, website, and web server at CERN. The next year, around December, the first web server outside Europe was developed and installed at SLAC in California. As time went on and everything continued to progress, the growth was small but noticeable for the number of Web servers and browsers by late 1992.


Part of it's earlier days

In April of 1993, CERN began releasing the World Wide Web into the public domain and made it a standard, which led to its widespread adoption. This spread over time allowed it to evolve, and by 1993, there were around 500 known web servers, and the World Wide Web accounted for 1% of internet traffic.


Even in the early days of its development, but later in the timeline, it was shown that there was rapid growth in the number of servers available. In December of 1994, there were over 10,000 Web servers available for use, of which 2,000 were commercial, and 10 million users.
As for the status of web servers, they have advanced significantly compared to how they operated in the past. Web servers nowadays aren’t really limited to running on a single physical machine like they used to be.

The single type of web server created back in 1990 at CERN differs drastically from how usable servers have evolved today. Even with all the advancements in how they operate, their purpose remains the same: receiving user requests and delivering web content that accurately and reliably matches their searches. The way these web servers evolved has made this web-related technology one of the most important on the World Wide Web.
